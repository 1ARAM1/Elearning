A1-A2: Beginner Level

Comparative and Superlative Adjectives

Comparative adjectives compare two things (e.g., bigger, smarter), while superlative adjectives show the highest degree of something (e.g., the biggest, the most beautiful).

Examples:

  • John is taller than Peter. (Comparative)
  • This is the most interesting book I've read. (Superlative)

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. My house is _____ (big) than yours.
  2. This is the _____ (good) movie I’ve ever seen.
Some / Any / No

We use "some" for positive sentences, "any" for questions and negative sentences, and "no" for total negation.

Examples:

  • There is some milk in the fridge. (Positive)
  • Do you have any pens? (Question)
  • There are no apples left. (Negative)

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. We don’t have _____ bread left.
  2. Are there _____ cookies in the jar?
Much / Many / A lot of / Few / Little

Use "much" with uncountable nouns and "many" with countable nouns. "A lot of" works for both. "Few" and "little" refer to small amounts, but "few" is for countable nouns and "little" is for uncountable nouns.

Examples:

  • There isn’t much water left.
  • How many books do you have?
  • She has a lot of friends.
  • There are few students in the classroom.
  • We have little time before the meeting starts.

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. How _____ money do you need for the trip?
  2. We have _____ milk left in the fridge.

A2-B1: Intermediate Level

Present Simple vs. Present Continuous

We use Present Simple for routines and facts, and Present Continuous for actions happening right now.

Examples:

  • I go to the gym every morning. (Present Simple)
  • I am studying right now. (Present Continuous)

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. She _____ (read) a book at the moment. (Present Continuous)
  2. We _____ (go) to the park every Sunday. (Present Simple)

B1-B1+: Upper Intermediate Level

Second Conditional

We use the Second Conditional to talk about hypothetical or imaginary situations in the present or future.

Example:

  • If I were you, I would travel the world.

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. If I _____ (be) rich, I would buy a big house.

Advanced Level

Modal Verbs

Modal verbs express necessity, possibility, permission, or ability. Common modal verbs include can, could, must, might, should, and would.

Examples:

  • You should study harder. (Advice)
  • She can swim very well. (Ability)
  • They might come tomorrow. (Possibility)

Exercise:

Fill in the blanks:

  1. You _____ (must) be more careful.
  2. He _____ (can) speak three languages.
